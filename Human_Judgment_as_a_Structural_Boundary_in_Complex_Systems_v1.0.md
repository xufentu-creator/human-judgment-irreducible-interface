## Statement on Text Nature and Research Scope

This work is an interdisciplinary, structural study concerned with the stability boundaries faced by interpretive systems and automated systems under conditions of high complexity, uncertainty, and irreversibility. It does not offer ontological conclusions regarding human nature, ideology, or the origin of consciousness or “soul,” and it does not constitute any normative guidance, governance proposal, or technical roadmap selection.

The purpose of this study is to use analytical perspectives from information theory and complex systems dynamics to delineate the applicable boundaries of interpretive systems under real-world conditions, and, on that basis, to discuss the structural position of judgment and responsibility in long-run system operation. This work does not rely on any specific technological form, institutional arrangement, religious tradition, or era-specific context. Its conclusions are methodological and structural in nature.

This work does not require readers to accept any unified worldview, nor does it seek to close interpretive space around the related questions. The discussion is offered solely to clarify the structural constraints that complex systems inevitably face when interpretive closure cannot be formed.

This work provides conceptual and structural analysis only. It does not constitute legal, policy, engineering, safety, compliance, or other professional advice. Any application must be evaluated by the reader in context, with responsibility assumed by the reader.

## I. Research Background and Problem Definition

### I.1 The Continued Expansion of Interpretive Capacity

Across scientific research, engineering practice, and computational development, human beings have continuously expanded the capacity to describe, model, and predict complex phenomena. Through statistical methods, formal models, and computational tools, an increasing range of processes has been brought into domains that are interpretable, reproducible, and executable.

This expansion has markedly improved efficiency and reliability across many fields and has provided important tools for understanding complex phenomena. At the same time, it raises a structural question: whether increased interpretive capacity implies that a system can achieve self-consistent closure at all levels of operation.

From a methodological standpoint, “the expansion of interpretive capacity” includes at least three distinct shifts. First, the scope of interpretive targets expands: more kinds of phenomena are transformed into representable variables and operable model relations. Second, interpretive time horizons extend: systems move from explaining past events to increasingly forecasting future states and pre-adjusting action paths. Third, the functional position of interpretation migrates: models are no longer used solely for understanding and explanation, but become embedded in decision workflows as key intermediaries for resource allocation, risk assessment, and action selection.

These shifts transform interpretive systems from “cognitive tools” into parts of operational structure. Once embedded, outputs are no longer merely descriptive; they tend to have real consequences. Even when a system does not explicitly treat model outputs as commands, the model’s position in the process can alter direction, speed, and priority. This implies that effectiveness is no longer measured only by interpretive accuracy, but also by the functional role interpretation plays within the workflow.

In high-complexity environments, the core limitation facing interpretive systems is not whether more complex models can be produced, but whether the object of interpretation itself is incomplete, irreversible, and subject to external disturbances that cannot be fully controlled. Even when models are locally reproducible and predictive, systems may still encounter interpretive boundaries under cross-scale coupling, extreme perturbations, or long-run path dependence. Such boundaries do not necessarily appear as “model error.” More often, they appear as the inability of a model to provide sufficient conditions for decision closure—forcing systems to act while information remains incomplete.

Accordingly, the expansion of interpretive capacity poses a structural question: when interpretive systems continue entering operational structure and assuming higher-level functions, will systems equate “stronger interpretation” with “achieved closure”? Later sections argue that interpretive systems have irreducible boundaries under real-world conditions, and that long-run stability is not determined by interpretive capacity alone, but by how a system handles judgment, responsibility, and consequence-bearing when interpretive closure is absent.

### I.2 Tension Between Interpretive Capacity and Responsibility Structure

Although interpretive systems can describe state transitions and probability distributions, they do not themselves bear the real-world consequences produced by decisions and actions. Under conditions of high uncertainty and irreversibility, decision impacts unfold over time and may involve cascading effects that cannot be fully anticipated.

Structurally, an interpretive system’s core function is to provide descriptions and evaluations of possible states, typically in the form of probabilities, intervals, or model assumptions. Such outputs can support decision processes, but they do not constitute mechanisms for consequence-bearing. Even when accuracy is high, what is offered is conditional information rather than a commitment to future states.

In real operations, once embedded into decision workflows, interpretive outputs are frequently treated as grounds for action. Yet grounds are not the same as bearing. Interpretation can explain why a choice has higher success probability under stated conditions, but it cannot be responsible for deviations and losses when conditions change, assumptions fail, or extreme events occur. This is not a defect; it follows from the interpretive system’s functional positioning.

When interpretive, predictive, and execution capacities expand while responsibility and the positioning of judgment are not made explicit, systems face risks of blurred accountability, unclear consequence-bearing, and weakened long-run trust structure. These risks may not appear as a single failed decision; they more often accumulate through repeated responsibility transfer, dilution, or deferral—reducing traceability of a system’s own consequences.

Moreover, under irreversibility, consequences cannot be fully eliminated by post-hoc correction. Interpretive systems can provide after-the-fact analysis and model updates, but they cannot undo what has already unfolded. Without an explicit consequence-bearing location in structure, stronger interpretation may even intensify responsibility suspension, leaving a system without stable mechanisms for bearing long-run outcomes.

This tension is not a matter of value judgment or ethical dispute, but a structurally analyzable vacancy: a gap generated by the expansion of interpretive function without a synchronized evolution of responsibility structure. Its existence does not depend on any specific technology or institution; it depends on whether a system, when interpretive closure is absent, has made a bearing position explicit.

### I.3 A Structural Formulation of the Research Question

Based on the above, this work asks:

Under conditions of incomplete information, irreversible processes, and high complexity, do interpretive systems face structural boundaries that cannot be eliminated by further internal expansion? If such boundaries exist, does long-run stability depend on an irreducible position for judgment and consequence-bearing that cannot be fully derived from interpretive systems?

This question does not deny interpretive capability. It examines its applicability conditions. In real environments, interpretive expansion often targets increased precision, compute capacity, or forecast range, but these enhancements do not necessarily produce structural closure. Even as interpretive capability strengthens, systems may still face incomplete information, time constraints, or abrupt environmental change at critical decision points.

Here, “structural boundary” does not refer to limitations of a particular model, but to constraints that interpretive systems share under real operational conditions. Such constraints cannot be removed by a single discipline or technical path. Their presence does not depend on whether a system is “advanced,” but on whether action must be taken before uncertainty is resolved.

Accordingly, the focus shifts from “whether interpretation is sufficient” to “how a system continues operating when interpretation is not sufficient.” This shift moves analysis from improving interpretive capacity to the stability conditions of operation under non-closure.

Thus, the research question can be restated: when interpretive systems cannot provide fully closed conditions for decision, must a structurally irreducible position be introduced to bear judgment and its unfolded consequences? If that position is absent, does the system face long-run stability risks?

This work treats the above as structural and methodological, not as a normative assessment of human value, technical direction, or social form. It makes no “ought” claims and presupposes no governance goal. The discussion is offered solely to analyze basic constraints faced by complex systems under real conditions.

## II. Interpretive Limits from an Information-Theoretic Perspective

### II.1 Information, Uncertainty, and the Scope of Interpretation

Information theory provides formal tools for analyzing uncertainty, noise, and reliable transmission under constraints. Within its scope, it can describe distributional change and capacity limits. Information, in this framework, measures uncertainty about states; the objects are symbols, signals, and statistical properties. Through coding, compression, and error correction, information theory evaluates achievable efficiency and error bounds under given constraints—fundamental to communications, control, and modeling.

However, these tools describe how states can be represented and transmitted; they do not prescribe how decisions are made or borne. Information theory can indicate conditions for minimizing loss, but it does not address whether action should occur when information remains incomplete, nor how decision consequences are borne.

Structurally, this is not a flaw; it is a boundary of theoretical function. Information theory typically assumes that decision subjects and consequence-bearing structures already exist, and then optimizes uncertainty handling within that structure. When this assumption does not hold—when action must occur before closure—information theory itself does not specify a “judgment interface.”

Thus, in complex systems, information theory can formalize how uncertainty changes, but it cannot answer how a system continues operating when uncertainty cannot be further compressed. This gap is not closed by more complex coding or more compute; it reflects a difference in structural roles between information handling and consequence-bearing judgment.

### II.2 Noise, Entropy, and Incompleteness

In high-noise, high-complexity environments, information cannot be fully compressed and interpretation is inevitably incomplete. Real decisions must occur while information remains non-closed.

Here, noise is not merely random channel disturbance; it includes any factor causing bias or loss in acquisition, representation, or interpretation—measurement limits, environmental drift, nonlinear interaction, or high-dimensional state spaces. Such factors prevent complete description of relevant variables under finite time and resource constraints.

Entropy quantifies uncertainty. In idealized settings, modeling and updating can reduce entropy, but in high-complexity systems, new uncertainty is continuously generated during evolution, so entropy reduction is not monotonic. Even if local uncertainty is compressed, overall uncertainty may remain high due to perturbations or internal coupling.

Incompleteness is therefore not temporary; it is normal. Interpretation can form effective local models, but these rely on implicit assumptions. When systems cross assumption boundaries or when model applicability conflicts, incompleteness becomes explicit. This does not necessarily indicate error; it indicates a basic constraint of real conditions.

Accordingly, interpretive systems cannot form fully self-consistent closure across all conditions. Even as interpretation strengthens, zones of irreducible uncertainty remain. Systems cannot avoid incomplete decision conditions by infinitely extending interpretation; decision is not an optional step after interpretation finishes, but an unavoidable intervention while interpretation remains non-closed. This provides the theoretical basis for later analysis of judgment as an interface.

### II.3 Structural Observation

When interpretive closure cannot be formed, stability no longer depends only on stronger interpretation, but on how judgment and its consequences are borne under incomplete conditions. This bearing position is not a direct product of information processing; it is a structural boundary revealed by interpretive systems under real conditions.

This observation does not deny interpretive efficacy; it limits its role. Interpretive systems can optimize representation and prediction under assumptions, but when systems continuously generate new uncertainty, interpretation cannot guarantee closed decision conditions. In such cases, merely extending interpretation cannot eliminate reliance on judgment.

From an operational standpoint, stability is not identical to ever-higher predictive precision. It depends on whether the system has a structural support that allows continued operation under non-closure. When interpretation cannot provide fully closed decision conditions, a bearing position must connect interpretation and action so that decisions can occur and consequences can be absorbed over time.

This bearing position cannot be reduced to information processing, because information processing concerns state description and uncertainty management, while bearing judgment involves accepting incompleteness and assuming the unfolding of consequences. These functions occupy distinct structural roles and cannot be merged by increasing model complexity.

Therefore, the bearing position is not an undeveloped internal module of interpretation. It is a boundary position that reappears throughout long-run operation as a stability condition. This observation grounds later discussion of judgment as an irreducible interface and clarifies the applicability boundary of interpretive systems in complex environments.

## III. Complex Systems Dynamics and the Capability Boundaries of Self-Organization

### III.1 Self-Organization and Emergence in Complex Systems

Complex systems dynamics studies how systems composed of many interacting units form global structures and stable patterns under nonlinearity. Self-organization and emergence show that macroscopic order can arise through local interaction without centralized control.

In this framework, global behavior is not a linear sum of parts; it appears over time through many local interactions. Self-organization emphasizes that rules need not be externally imposed; they can form under internal constraints. This enables systems to maintain some structural stability in open environments and provides powerful lenses for biological, ecological, and social systems.

However, self-organization does not imply unlimited stability. Stable regimes typically depend on parameter ranges and environmental conditions; outside those ranges, structures may collapse or shift to new regimes—common under nonlinearity.

Dynamically, self-organization explains how structures form, not how they persist under all conditions. Feedback can absorb perturbations within ranges, but once perturbations cross thresholds or systems enter new regimes, self-organization does not guarantee continued stability.

Thus, self-organization and emergence reveal how complex systems form order without centralized control, but they do not remove structural boundaries under high complexity, uncertainty, and irreversibility. Long-run stability depends not only on rules and feedback, but on how a system continues operating when existing rules no longer sufficiently apply—providing a foundation for later analysis.

### III.2 Nonlinearity, Uncertainty, and Irreversible Processes

High-complexity systems often evolve nonlinearly, are sensitive to initial conditions, and exhibit path dependence. Once states change, systems may not return to prior states, producing irreversibility.

Nonlinearity means outputs are not proportional to inputs. Small perturbations may be amplified and propagate through coupling. Path dependence intensifies uncertainty: different early choices can push systems into different trajectories that become difficult to reverse.

In such environments, local disturbances can trigger cross-scale effects and consequences cannot be reliably inferred by simple extrapolation. This is structural, not merely due to weak models. Even with refined models, parameter drift, environmental change, or accumulated interaction can shift systems into new regimes, where previous assumptions no longer hold. This “failure” indicates boundary crossing, not necessarily model error.

Irreversibility is thus normal, not exceptional. Once an irreversible process unfolds, post-hoc updates can explain but cannot erase consequences. This highlights a key constraint: systems must operate under incompleteness in time, and stability cannot be reduced to rules and prediction alone.

### III.3 Structural Limits of Dynamical Rules

While dynamical models can explain how structures form, they cannot answer who bears the real consequences of structural failure. Dynamical rules describe how states change; they do not specify how judgment is made before change occurs, nor how responsibility is allocated over time.

Rules constrain transitions and describe possible regimes under conditions. They are effective for structure formation and phase transitions. Yet their premise is formalizable state description; they do not contain mechanisms of consequence-bearing.

Under uncertainty and irreversibility, reliance on dynamical rules alone cannot guarantee long-run stability. The core issue is not efficiency, but the positioning of responsibility and judgment. Once thresholds are crossed, consequences unfold and cannot be removed by rule adjustment or path rollback. Models can explain afterward, but they do not provide a bearing position beforehand.

Thus, complex systems contain a structural vacancy that dynamics alone cannot fill. This vacancy persists because dynamical rules focus on evolution logic, while judgment and responsibility concern bearing path selection under incompleteness. These roles differ in object and temporal direction and cannot be merged by adding rule complexity.

This does not deny dynamical models’ value; it delineates their real-world boundary. At this boundary, stability questions shift from “how evolution occurs” to “how consequences are borne,” opening the interface for later discussion of judgment as an irreducible position.

## IV. Judgment as an Irreducible System Interface

### IV.1 Structural Definition of Judgment

In this work, judgment is not treated as cognitive capacity, intelligence level, or moral quality. It is defined as a system interface—an interface that bears decision under incomplete information and provides a traceable bearing location for consequences as they unfold over time.

This emphasizes structural function rather than psychological or cultural attributes. Judgment, in this sense, is an interface condition required for stable operation.

Structurally, an interface is not identical to an internal processing unit; it is a position that connects functional layers. Judgment as interface does not generate new information or optimize computation; it establishes a locatable connection point between interpretation and action so that operation can continue when information is non-closed.

Earlier analysis shows interpretive systems and rule mechanisms face structural boundaries under high complexity, uncertainty, and irreversibility. Judgment appears at these boundaries. Its existence is not driven by a need for “higher intelligence,” but by unavoidable real-world conditions in which decisions must occur under incompleteness.

Functionally, judgment does not eliminate uncertainty; it accepts uncertainty as a condition for decision. What judgment provides is not more complete explanation, but a structural condition for bearing consequences when explanation is insufficient. This distinction makes judgment irreducible to information processing or rule execution.

Judgment is defined as an interface rather than a module because its role does not disappear as complexity increases. On the contrary, as uncertainty and irreversibility intensify, the interface role becomes more explicit. This interface is not tied to any specific architecture; it reappears as a structural position across systems.

Accordingly, judgment is treated here as an irreducible system interface. Its irreducibility does not come from psychological complexity or cultural difference, but from structural constraints faced by systems in real operation.

### IV.2 Judgment and Incomplete Decision Conditions

In real environments, decisions must often be made before information becomes fully determined. Such decisions cannot be avoided merely by delaying action, because the environment evolves under time constraints.

Decisions are embedded in ongoing change rather than occurring after a static “information collection phase.” Waiting for full closure is frequently infeasible; delay may introduce new risks or cause loss of actionable windows. In such contexts, the question is not whether to decide, but under what incompleteness to decide.

Judgment appears under these conditions. It does not fill missing information; it enables decision under non-closure and maintains a traceable bearing path for unfolding consequences.

Structurally, information processing maximizes usable information within a time window; judgment addresses what happens when the time window itself is a binding constraint. Their temporal direction differs: interpretation seeks more sufficiency; judgment accepts insufficiency and bears consequences.

Thus, judgment is not driven by the pursuit of higher precision, but by time constraint and irreversibility. Whenever systems must act before change stops, judgment as interface necessarily appears.

### IV.3 The Non-Derivability of Judgment

Because judgment occurs where interpretive closure cannot be formed, it cannot be derived through information compression or dynamical rule deduction. Judgment is not an output of interpretation; it is an external interface on which interpretive systems rely under real uncertainty.

In information theory, derivability implies a stable mapping from states to outputs under formal constraints. Under noise, incompleteness, and time pressure, such mappings cannot be fully established across decision contexts. In dynamics, derivability depends on rules remaining valid across state space; under nonlinearity and regime shifts, rules lose binding power and cannot specify a bearing position for path entry.

Judgment’s non-derivability is therefore not due to insufficient model complexity, but due to structural constraints. Judgment does not add compute; it accepts non-closure and provides continuity for consequence-bearing over time.

If judgment is misframed as a derivable output, systems tend to attribute consequences to models or rules, thereby obscuring the bearing position. This may not cause immediate error but leads to responsibility fracture and suspended consequences over time. In this sense, irreducibility is a stability condition rather than a theoretical exception.

## V. Temporal Continuity and the Constraint of Responsibility-Bearing

### V.1 The Temporal Unfolding of Decision Consequences

Consequences triggered by judgment do not complete instantly; they unfold over time across stages and interact with later decisions and environmental change.

Once a decision occurs, it initiates an influence path that evolves over time. This path is not linear; it interweaves with later state changes, disturbances, and other decisions. As a result, consequences cannot be fully evaluated at the moment of judgment; they become visible only as time advances.

In complex systems, effects accumulate. Early judgments constrain later option spaces and influence trajectories. Stability therefore depends not on whether single judgments are “correct,” but on whether unfolding consequences remain traceable and continuously absorbable. If bearing positions vanish over time, systems face responsibility fracture and path loss.

### V.2 Necessity of a Continuous Bearing Position

Responsibility depends on continuity of the bearing position over time. If the position is not identifiable, traceable, or consistent, judgment outcomes lose a clear bearing subject.

Responsibility is not instantaneous; it is a structural relation maintained across time. If the bearing position is fragmented, replaced, or dissolved, the system cannot stably link later effects to the original judgment.

In high-complexity systems, when judgment is fully sliced, anonymized, or distributed without continuity, long-run trust structure is weakened. This is not about one failure; it is about structural discontinuity. Local efficiency may remain, but time-scale accountability dissipates.

A continuous bearing position does not require a specific implementation; it requires identifiability and temporal consistency—so that starting points, unfolding paths, and consequences remain linkable over time.

### V.3 Structural Constraint Clarification

These constraints do not arise from ethical claims; they arise from the temporal nature of complex systems. If judgment loses a continuous bearing position, consequences cannot be stably absorbed, and overall operation is affected.

Time is not neutral background; it is the dimension in which systems evolve and consequences unfold. Without a consistent bearing position, effects detach from origins and become fragmented, producing gradual instability. Short-term efficiency can coexist with long-term interpretive rupture and rising unpredictability.

Continuous bearing is therefore not about strengthening control; it is about preserving minimal causal linkage for long-run stability. The requirement does not specify who should bear responsibility; it states that without a continuously identifiable bearing position, stability degrades.

## VI. Boundary Consistency Across Theoretical Frameworks

### VI.1 Scope of Explanation in Biology and Systems Science

Biology and systems science effectively explain how structures, behavior patterns, and adaptation mechanisms form. They provide strong bases for understanding complex systems through genetics, metabolism, feedback, networks, and dynamics.

Yet these explanations largely focus on how structures form, not how judgment occurs under non-closure, nor how responsibility is positioned. They assume decision/response mechanisms exist and then describe how they operate. Under incomplete information, irreversibility, and time constraints, these theories can explain state change, but they do not supply a bearing position for judgment.

This is not theoretical weakness; it is scope boundary. They explain how systems become what they are; judgment interfaces concern how systems continue operating when explanation cannot close. Defining judgment as an irreducible interface does not conflict with these theories; it clarifies their applicability boundaries and adds a complementary structural note at their edges.

### VI.2 Applicability Boundaries of Rational Explanation

Scientific rationality is powerful within its methodological scope and also recognizes that some problems exceed derivability and verifiability. When explanation reaches boundary conditions, expanding models does not remove structural incompleteness.

Rational explanation relies on observable data, repeatable processes, and formal derivations. In complex systems under uncertainty, nonlinearity, and time pressure, some decision contexts cannot be fully captured within verifiable frameworks. Model expansion can improve local description but cannot change the fact that decisions must occur under time constraint before closure.

Science itself acknowledges this through approximations, assumptions, and validity intervals. These constraints are not weakness; they are reliability foundations.

Thus, when interpretive boundaries are reached, the problem is not “explanation is insufficient,” but “explanation cannot bear closure.” Judgment as interface is not anti-rational; it supplements functional positioning: explanation describes and predicts; judgment bears non-closure decisions and unfolding consequences.

### VI.3 Structural Observation of Boundary Consistency

Across biology, systems science, information theory, and complex dynamics, distinct frameworks encounter boundaries around judgment and responsibility. Their languages differ, but structurally they converge: there exists a judgment position that cannot be fully absorbed by explanation mechanisms.

This is not due to mutual borrowing; it arises from shared real-world constraints: incomplete information, time pressure, and irreversible consequences. Under these conditions, explanation necessarily faces stop-points.

Accordingly, judgment as interface is not bound to one tradition; it is a structural position appearing at the edge of multiple theories. It does not require unifying languages or merging concepts; it requires recognizing a minimal shared interface layer for discussing stability constraints.

## VII. Boundary Handling on Subject and Consciousness Questions

### VII.1 Explicit Non-Ontological Stance

This work adopts a non-ontological stance in discussing judgment, responsibility, and stability. It does not define what the subject is, what consciousness is, or how “soul” exists, nor does it explain origins, mechanisms, or hierarchies.

This is not avoidance but methodological delimitation. Such questions vary across philosophy, neuroscience, religion, and cultures, often involving non-falsifiable premises. Bringing them into this framework would blur the structural focus.

The work does not deny importance; it places these questions outside its interpretive scope. The analysis concerns not what a subject “is,” but whether a system under non-closure needs a locatable interface that can bear consequences over time.

Thus, judgment is not bound to any particular theory of subjectivity or consciousness. Whether different frameworks interpret it as “subject,” “agency,” or otherwise is their internal matter; it is not a prerequisite here.

### VII.2 Structural Meaning of Explanation Stop Conditions

Under high complexity and incompleteness, interpretive systems inevitably face stop conditions. Expanding models does not eliminate all uncertainty and may even obscure the judgment interface on which real operation relies.

Every explanatory system has implicit stop conditions—where it ceases to expand and acknowledges applicability limits. Without such conditions, explanation risks misuse. In complex systems, endless model expansion can appear complete while structurally misplacing judgment and responsibility.

Treating subject and consciousness questions as stop-conditions helps prevent misclassifying irreducible structure as a “technical gap.” It protects the judgment interface as a bearing position rather than forcing it into a derivable module.

### VII.3 Neutrality of Boundary Handling

The boundary handling above depends on no religious, philosophical, or ideological premise. It is based only on operational constraints of complex systems under incomplete information, time limits, and irreversible consequences.

Its purpose is to preserve coexistence among explanatory frameworks, not to restrict them. By clarifying what belongs inside explanatory scope and what functions as stop-conditions, the analysis avoids converting structural issues into worldview disputes.

This neutrality does not exclude any tradition; it provides a minimal shared operational premise for structural discussion. As long as a framework acknowledges incompleteness and time constraint, how it interprets subjectivity or meaning does not alter the structural conclusions offered here.

## VIII. Judgment Sovereignty as a Stability Condition

### VIII.1 Structural Meaning of Judgment Sovereignty

In this work, “judgment sovereignty” means that judgment and responsibility cannot be fully outsourced to interpretive or automated systems. This is not political or legal sovereignty. It is a structural stability condition.

Here, “sovereignty” is not a power claim; it is a functional boundary: judgment as interface must retain an independent bearing position in system structure.

Judgment sovereignty is not a superiority claim about judgment capacity; it is a factual description that the bearing position cannot be absorbed by the system without stability costs.

When judgment and responsibility are fully folded into interpretation or execution chains, systems may gain short-term coherence and efficiency, but under incomplete information, irreversible consequences, and time limits, they lose traceable bearing positions. This loss appears over time, not necessarily immediately.

Thus, judgment sovereignty is not a restriction on system capability; it clarifies stability conditions. It does not specify how judgment is executed or who bears it; it states only that judgment and consequence-bearing cannot be entirely transferred into interpretation or automation.

**In this work, “sovereignty” is used strictly as a structural boundary term describing non-outsourcability, and does not constitute any rights claim, institutional stance, or governance advocacy.**

### VIII.2 Structural Distinction Between Judgment and Execution

Execution can be highly automated under stable conditions because it is repeatable and substitutable when inputs and rules are stable. Judgment occurs prior to execution and bears selection responsibility under non-closure.

If judgment is not structurally distinguished from execution, systems tend to misclassify judgment as repeatable execution, leading to blurred responsibility. The result is not necessarily immediate failure but progressive loss of traceability when irreversible consequences appear.

This distinction is not about efficiency; it is a prerequisite for locatable responsibility. Automation can fully cover execution, but judgment—bearing non-closure choices—must remain structurally independent.

### VIII.3 Stability Linkage Clarification

When judgment sovereignty is absent, systems may show short-term efficiency gains but face long-run responsibility fracture, weakened trust structure, and accumulation of unanticipated risk. This linkage arises from structure, not values.

Folding judgment into interpretation/execution reduces coordination costs under stable inputs. But under time pressure and irreversibility, systems lose stable linkage between outcomes and decision origins. Over time, explanatory capacity about consequences weakens.

Here, “trust” is not a psychological claim; it is a structural dependency on interpretability and traceability. Without clear judgment bearing points, anomalies are attributed to process/environment rather than identifiable decision interfaces, increasing accumulated risk.

This is not a design evaluation; it is a structural relationship between judgment sovereignty and long-run stability.

## IX. Judgment-Bearing in Digital Environments

### IX.1 Changes in the Environment Where Judgment Occurs

As information environments evolve, more judgment occurs in digital and abstract spaces. Judgment may no longer be paired with direct physical action, while consequences still unfold in real systems.

Traditionally, judgment is tied to physical acts and locations; in digital environments, it can occur via symbolic operations, data processing, or interface interactions. This abstraction reduces visibility and locatability, while consequences remain real through indirect effects on allocation, paths, and states.

Digital environments do not create a new judgment problem; they amplify the bearing problem: how judgment remains identifiable and responsibility remains continuous when judgment is abstracted.

### IX.2 The Structural Role of Digital Identity

In this work, “digital identity” is not a user account, legal identity, or access credential. It is defined as a structural interface for identifying and bearing judgment and its unfolding consequences in digital systems.

Its function is not to answer “who someone is,” but to answer “where judgment occurred and how it remains continuously linkable.” No implementation is assumed. The point is that without a bearing interface, systems cannot maintain consistent responsibility continuity when judgment becomes abstract.

This “digital identity” concept does not equate to legal subjecthood. It is a structural position preventing judgment from being fully fragmented, anonymized, or absorbed into indistinguishable processing flows.

### IX.3 Non-Normative and Implementation-Neutral Statement

This work does not propose design principles, technical standards, or governance models for digital identity. The discussion is offered only to clarify the structural constraint: if judgment lacks a continuously identifiable bearing position in digital environments, long-run stability is affected.

Pointing out a constraint is not prescribing a solution. Any concrete solution depends on era-specific technology and governance context and lies beyond this work. Implementation neutrality preserves exploration space while ensuring the bearing constraint remains visible as a structural condition rather than being misframed as a purely technical optimization task.

## X. Discussion and Research Limits

### X.1 Theoretical Nature and Applicability

This is a structural and methodological study. Its conclusions apply to systems characterized by high complexity, incomplete information, and irreversibility. In low-complexity, fully automatable, or closed systems, the constraints discussed here may not hold or may not be salient.

This work does not provide empirical modeling or experimental validation for specific systems. It identifies boundary patterns that recur under certain operational conditions. It is not about performance or optimal solutions, but about minimal structural conditions for long-run stability.

### X.2 Unaddressed Questions

This work does not analyze psychological mechanisms, neural bases, or cultural formation of judgment, nor does it discuss concrete system design. These topics belong to their respective domains and remain open for further research.

This limitation is deliberate: judgment is analyzed here as a structural interface, not as an individual-level mechanism. Concrete design proposals would require era-specific assumptions and would violate the non-normative, implementation-neutral stance of this work.

### X.3 Future Research Space

The structural boundary identified here can serve as an analytic starting point for subsequent interdisciplinary research on how judgment and responsibility are borne under different system conditions.

Future studies may connect systems science, information studies, cognition research, and social system analysis without requiring a unified worldview. They may also examine how different technological or organizational contexts influence visibility and continuity of judgment bearing—without treating this work as a normative authority.

## XI. Conclusion

From the perspectives of information theory and complex systems dynamics, this work analyzes structural boundaries faced by interpretive systems under real conditions, and argues that in high-complexity, incomplete environments, judgment—defined as an irreducible interface—has foundational significance for long-run system stability.

By examining information incompleteness, nonlinear evolution, irreversible processes, and constraints of temporal continuity, this work shows that regardless of how far interpretive capacity expands, systems inevitably encounter interpretive stop-points around judgment and responsibility under uncertainty and time constraints. These stop-points are not caused by inadequate theory or immature technology, but by structural constraints of real operation.

Accordingly, judgment is defined here as a system interface whose function is to bear decisions under non-closure and to provide an identifiable, continuous bearing position for consequences as they unfold over time. Judgment is not treated as psychological capacity or value claim, but as a structural condition for stability.

Further, through analysis of judgment sovereignty, the distinction between judgment and execution, and judgment bearing in digital environments, this work clarifies that the structural significance of judgment does not disappear in highly automated systems. Instead, it becomes more explicit when bearing paths are more prone to fracture under abstraction.

This work does not reach conclusions about human essence, consciousness, or “soul,” and it advances no normative claims. It intentionally maintains a non-ontological, non-normative, and implementation-neutral stance to avoid converting structural analysis into worldview disputes.

The core contribution is to delineate the applicability boundary of interpretive systems and to clarify the structural position of judgment and responsibility in complex systems. This boundary framing preserves space for diverse theoretical approaches while providing an independently citable structural reference for future research and system analysis.

In an environment of continually expanding interpretation and automation, this work attempts to preserve an irreducible position that cannot be fully absorbed by the system—so that when systems confront non-closure decisions and irreversible, time-unfolding consequences, they retain a minimal structural prerequisite for stable operation. This position does not point to any specific subject, capacity, or implementation form; it exists only as the judgment interface required for continued operation under real constraints.

### A. Terminology Notes

To reduce ambiguity, key terms are defined as follows. These terms are used only within the context of this work and do not claim universal meanings across other theories, technologies, or practices.

**Interpretive System**

Any theory, model, or formal framework used to describe, model, predict, or explain system state transitions, including but not limited to information-theoretic models, complex dynamics rules, or computational derivation structures. No specific implementation or application domain is assumed.

**Judgment**

Defined here as a system interface that bears decision under incomplete information and time constraints, and provides an identifiable bearing position for the temporal unfolding of consequences. Judgment is not treated as psychological capacity, moral attribute, or value stance.

**Judgment Sovereignty**

A structural fact that judgment and responsibility bearing cannot be fully absorbed into interpretive systems or automated execution mechanisms. This term is not political/legal sovereignty; it is strictly a structural boundary description.

**Bearing Position**

A structural position that continuously links the origin of judgment, its evolving path, and unfolding consequences. The position requires identifiability and temporal continuity, not any particular implementation.

**Digital Identity**

A structural interface in digital environments used to identify and bear judgment and its consequences, distinct from user accounts, legal identity, or social identity. No design or governance model is proposed.

### B. Non-Goals

To avoid misunderstanding, this work explicitly does not aim to:

1. Define or explain the ontological nature of subject, consciousness, or “soul.”
2. Propose ethical norms, governance principles, or policy recommendations.
3. Provide system design solutions, technical standards, or implementation paths.
4. Compare, adjudicate, or unify religions, philosophies, or ideologies.
5. Claim that judgment must be borne by any specific subject, group, or role.

These non-goals reflect deliberate methodological boundary setting to keep the analysis structural rather than normative.

---

### C. Compatibility with Existing Frameworks

This work does not replace or negate existing theories. It is intended to remain compatible at their explanatory boundaries.

**Relation to Information Theory**

Information-theoretic concepts are used within their appropriate scope. The judgment interface is placed outside information theory’s explanatory domain and does not diminish its validity.

**Relation to Complex Systems Dynamics**

Self-organization, emergence, and dynamical rules are recognized as central for explaining structure formation. Their boundaries around irreversible decision and responsibility bearing are noted. Judgment is treated as a complementary structural position, not a substitute.

**Relation to Biology, Systems Science, and Related Theories**

No new hypotheses about biological mechanisms or adaptation are proposed. The work only notes that judgment and responsibility bearing lie outside the primary aims of these frameworks under certain conditions.

**Relation to Philosophy, Cognitive Science, and Social Theory**

No subject theory, philosophy of mind, or normative social theory is advanced. Different theoretical paths may use this work as a structural reference without changing their own premises.
